The Quick Answer (If You're in a Hurry)

Don't have time for the details? Here are the fastest ways to get your table.

  • The Simple Copy & Paste: Go ahead and try the obvious first. Highlight the table with your mouse, hit Ctrl+C (Cmd+C on Mac), and paste it into your spreadsheet. For simple tables, this often works surprisingly well.
  • Ask Perplexity for a Better Format: If the simple paste fails, just ask Perplexity to help. Type a follow-up prompt like, "Please provide this table in Markdown format." This clean text format often pastes into spreadsheets much more cleanly. HTML is another great option.

A Deeper Look: Mastering Table Extraction

While the quick methods work a lot of the time, understanding your options will save you a ton of headaches down the road. Let's break down each method so you know exactly what to do when things go wrong.

Method 1: The Classic Copy and Paste

This is everyone's first move for a reasonâ€”it's fast and intuitive. But success often depends on the hidden complexity of the table.

How to Do It:

  1. Generate the Table: Ask your question and have Perplexity format the answer as a table.
  2. Select: Use your mouse to click and drag over the entire table, making sure every row and column is highlighted.
  3. Copy: Press Ctrl+C or right-click and select "Copy."
  4. Paste: Open Excel, Google Sheets, or Word and press Ctrl+V.

Why It Sometimes Fails:
The most common problem is formatting loss. When you copy from a webpage, you're not just copying the text you see; you're copying invisible HTML code that structures the data. Spreadsheets can get confused by this code, causing them to dump everything into one column. You might also accidentally miss a row or column when selecting, leading to incomplete data.

Method 2: Get Perplexity to Do the Work for You

When a direct copy fails, your next best move is to ask the AI to reformat the data into a more spreadsheet-friendly language. You don't need to be a programmer to use this trick.

  • HTML (HyperText Markup Language): This is the code that builds web pages. By asking for the table in HTML, you get the raw structure. You can use a free online "HTML to Excel" converter to get a perfect copy.
    • Your Prompt: "Can you give me that table in HTML format?"
  • Markdown: This is a much simpler markup language that uses plain text characters to define structure. It's incredibly clean and easy to work with. Pasting a Markdown table into many modern applications, including newer versions of Excel and Google Sheets (with add-ons), works flawlessly.
    • Your Prompt: "Please regenerate that table using Markdown."

This approach is powerful because you're removing the complex formatting and just keeping the essential data structure.
